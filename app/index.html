Contact dermatitis is a serious issue for healthcare workers
  • Healthcare workers are at high risk of developing occupational skin disease (OSD)1
  • Occupational contact dermatitis (OCD) accounts for 90% of all OSD2
17-50%
of nurses are affected by OCD1
  • Of healthcare workers with occupational contact dermatitis, almost 80% had irritant contact dermatitis (ICD) and 50% had allergic contact dermatitis (ACD)1
  • Occupational contact dermatitis is caused by daily exposure to irritants and hand hygiene requirements (‘wet’ environments)1

Causes of irritant contact dermatitis*1

*More than one factor may contribute.

Causes of allergic contact dermatitis (ACD)1

  • Rubber glove chemicals – thiuram mix and tetraethylthiuram disulfide
  • Preservatives – formaldehyde, formaldehyde releasers and isothiazolinones
  • Excipients in hand cleansers – hard-to-avoid, weak sensitisers
  • Antiseptics
ACD occurs more frequently with commercial hand cleansers than alcohol-based hand rubs.1

Personal and economic consequences

For healthcare workers:

  • Impaired quality of life 1,3,4
  • Inability to work 1,3,4

For medical facilities:1

  • Costs of medical care and sick leave
  • Lost productivity
  • Workers’ compensation claims

Guidelines to prevent and manage contact dermatitis1,2

1. Prevention1

  • Incorporate skin care advice into hand hygiene education
  • Encourage the use of alcohol-based hand rubs rather than ‘wet’ cleansers where appropriate
  • Substitute skin cleansers containing soap or weak allergens with soap - or allergen-free cleansers
  • Encourage regular, proactive use of moisturisers
  • Label potential irritants or allergens in products being used
  • Use accelerator-free gloves for healthcare workers prone to OCD

2. Management1

  • Skin care education and advice should be provided to all healthcare workers
  • Anyone with signs of OCD should seek early treatment
  • Alcohol-based hand rubs should be used instead of soap or antiseptic agents (unless the hands are visibly dirty / contaminated)
  • Good hand washing and drying techniques should be followed
  • Conditioning (barrier and/or moisturising) creams should be regularly used at work
  • Because skin affected by dermatitis may be colonised by bacteria, consider minimising contact with patients at high risk of hospital-acquired infection

Bucklands Lodge, North Plympton, SA

Bucklands Lodge is a high care facility in Adelaide with 147 residents.

After conducting a skin assessment on the residents and identifying that 75% were at risk of injury, the care team decided to monitor the frequency of skin tears over a six-month period.

At the end of this period, residents’ arms and legs were moisturised twice daily with QV Lotion for a further six months and the frequency of skin tears was again monitored.

Overall, there was a 31% reduction in skin tears.

Given this very pleasing result, all residents continue to receive twice-daily moisturising with QV – residents at low risk of skin tears use QV Lotion, those at high risk use QV Cream, and those with very dry skin use QV Bath Oil.

Willowbrooke Aged Care Facility, Ferntree Gully, Victoria

With 49 residents, Willowbrooke is a small-to-medium sized facility in Melbourne.

In August 2016, the care team reviewed skin tear incidents in the facility. Based on this, 20 residents who had a high incidence of skin tears were identified and invited to take part in a skin tear prevention trial.

Each resident’s arms and legs were moisturised twice daily with QV Lotion for a period of 3 months. Training was also provided to staff to help mitigate situations where skin tears can occur.

At the end of the trial, skin condition was improved in 85% of residents and there was a 27% reduction in skin tears.

It was also noted that when skin tears did occur, they tended to be less severe than those prior to the trial.

Baseline Period: 1st June to 30th August 2016
No. of Skin Tears: 22

Intervention Period: 1st September to 31st November 2016
Intervention: Moisturising arms and legs with QV Lotion twice daily
No. of Skin Tears: 16
